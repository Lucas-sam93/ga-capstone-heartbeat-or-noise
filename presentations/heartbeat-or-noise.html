The Central Challenge

The Modality Gap

Clinical ECG measures electrical cardiac activity. Consumer wearables measure optical blood volume (PPG). These are fundamentally different signals — the model was trained on one, validated on the other.

ECG (Training)
Electrical impulses, single-lead, clinical environment, 300 Hz
PPG (Wearable)
Optical pulse, motion-affected, ambient wear, 125 Hz

KS statistic quantifies this gap: how far the wearable feature distributions sit from the training distribution. KS > 0.3 = large gap. All p-values ~0.

Null Result — Three Explanations
1. Signal Modality Gap
5 of 8 features show large KS distances. Model is operating outside its training distribution.
2. Behavioural Confound
Reduced training intensity pre-anchor shifted HRV toward patterns the model reads as normal.
3. Abnormality Type Mismatch
ECG confirmed intraventricular conduction delay — a waveform morphology abnormality. HRV features measure beat-to-beat timing and are structurally incapable of detecting this.

Key insight: AUROC 0.86 confirms the model retains genuine discriminative ability across modalities. The specificity failure is a threshold calibration problem, not a model failure. Threshold 0.34 was calibrated on Physionet ECG — when wearable features systematically appear more abnormal, almost everything crosses the boundary. The same pattern occurs across all four models.

Threshold Recalibration

Adapting for Wearable Deployment

Fixed Threshold — 0.34
Sensitivity 100%
Specificity 12.5%

Calibrated on ECG data. Fails when wearable feature baseline is systematically shifted.

Youden's J — 0.8424
Sensitivity 73.7%
Specificity 93.8%

Specificity recovers to 93.8%. Threshold found and evaluated on same 35 subjects — overfit risk.

LOOCV Sens-Targeted — 0.8368
Sensitivity 78.9%
Specificity 81.2% ✓

Out-of-sample estimate. Specificity clears ≥75% criterion. Primary reported figure. Used in BeatCheck app.

Bootstrap AUROC (n=1,000)

95% CI: [0.7246, 0.9720] — lower bound confirms discriminative signal even in unlucky resamples.

+10.5pp sensitivity improvement at zero specificity cost — the right trade-off for a screening context.

Research Answer

Can Wearables Screen for Cardiac Anomalies?

Qualified Yes — with threshold recalibration
Evidence For
  • AUROC 0.86 on PPG confirms discriminative signal transfers
  • 100% AF sensitivity at fixed threshold — zero missed cases
  • LOOCV specificity 81.2% clears pre-registered criterion
  • Threshold recalibration recovers specificity to 93.8%
Limitations
  • N=35 validation — LOOCV sensitivity 78.9%, 1.1pp below criterion
  • Critically ill ICU population — not general population
  • Single PPG modality — conduction delays undetectable by HRV
  • Apple Watch null result documents these boundaries clearly
Next Steps
  • Larger multi-site wearable PPG validation study
  • Domain-adapted threshold for consumer wearable deployment
  • Multi-lead or hybrid feature approaches
  • Regulatory pathway considerations (Singapore market)
Recommendations

Four Actionable Next Steps

01

Domain-Adapted Threshold

Calibrate a wearable-specific decision threshold on a larger PPG dataset before any consumer deployment. The 0.34 ECG threshold is not portable — the 0.8368 LOOCV estimate is a provisional starting point, not a production threshold.

High Priority
02

Larger Multi-Site PPG Validation

N=35 is insufficient to confirm clinical utility. A study of 200+ subjects across multiple sites and wearable devices would tighten the AUROC confidence interval and reduce LOOCV variance on sensitivity.

Clinical Research
03

Multi-Modal or Hybrid Features

HRV-only features cannot detect morphology abnormalities like conduction delays. Incorporating accelerometer data, SpO2, or consumer ECG (Apple Watch Series 4+) would expand the detectable abnormality spectrum beyond rhythm irregularities.

Feature Engineering
04

Regulatory Pathway

For Singapore deployment, engagement with the Health Sciences Authority (HSA) is required. A software-as-a-medical-device (SaMD) framework assessment should determine whether BeatCheck requires Class A or Class B registration before any public rollout.
